## I. 🧠 Cognitive Science — Consciousness Is Not a Passive Receiver of Reality

Neuroscientist Anil Seth proposed a paradigm-shifting idea: **the brain is not passively receiving reality — it is actively predicting it.** Everything seen, heard, and felt is the brain's "best guess" generated from past experience, continuously refined by sensory input. In other words, everyone lives in a "controlled hallucination." When two people witness the same event and draw completely different conclusions, it is not because one is lying — their brains are simply running different predictive models.

This theory has a thought-provoking extension. When people talk about training AI agents, few realize that **humans are agents too.** A lifetime of experience is the prompt, the amount of information one can attend to at once is the context window, and judgments and actions are the output. Predictive processing turns this metaphor into testable science. Understanding it is understanding the "runtime architecture" of consciousness itself.

Everyday life offers countless validations of this theory. Why does the same type of content keep appearing? It is not the recommendation algorithm — the algorithm merely amplifies the brain's existing filtering mechanism. Opening a social platform, the eyes automatically skip content that does not fit the predictive model. Why does a change of environment spark creativity? A new setting temporarily disables predictive models, forcing the brain into learning mode, and the maximization of prediction error is precisely the source of creativity. Meditation works on the same principle: it is essentially training the brain not to trust its automatic predictions — observing without reacting severs the automated chain from prediction to action.

There is a more dangerous variant of the Dunning-Kruger effect: **the more expertise one has in a specific domain, the more likely they are to overestimate their judgment in unrelated fields.** Nobel laureates are more prone to making absurd statements outside their specialty; top engineers are more inclined toward overconfident macroeconomic predictions. This is not about intelligence — it is a blind spot in metacognition. Humans lack a built-in mechanism to flag "what they do not know."

Cross-disciplinary thinking is a valuable mental habit, but it requires installing a "I might not understand this" switch first. The story of Long-Term Capital Management in 1998 is a classic cautionary tale: two Nobel laureates in economics plus the smartest minds on Wall Street, running mathematical models for bond arbitrage, delivering 40% annualized returns for four years. Then Russia defaulted on its debt — an event their models had deemed so improbable as to be impossible — and the firm collapsed, nearly triggering a global financial crisis. The models were not wrong. The problem was that they did not know what their models did not know.

- 📖 Philip Tetlock, *Superforecasting: The Art and Science of Prediction*
- 📖 Nate Silver, *The Signal and the Noise*
- 🎯 A habit worth cultivating: ask once a day — "On which judgment today might I be wrong?"

## II. 🏛️ Philosophy — Stoicism's Surprising Relevance to Modern Life

Two thousand years ago, Epictetus said something remarkably simple: **"Some things are up to us, and some things are not up to us."** This is the foundation of Stoic philosophy — the dichotomy of control. What is up to us: judgment, attitude, choice of action. What is not up to us: the opinions of others, market fluctuations, aging, weather, trending topics.

Recognizing this framework reveals that most modern anxiety stems from trying to control the uncontrollable. Consuming industry news, tracking competitors, reading market reports — the content direction is up to the individual, but what goes viral, platform algorithms, audience size — none of these are. Drawing a clear line between the two removes more than half of all anxiety.

The Stoics pursued self-mastery. The AI era introduces a fascinating philosophical inversion: people are now pursuing systems where an agent makes judgments on their behalf. Where does this road lead? Closer to freedom, or further from it? There is no standard answer, but it is a question worth revisiting every time an automation system is designed.

The Stoics had a famous practice called **Premeditatio Malorum**: spend two minutes each morning imagining the worst that could happen that day — a rejected project, criticism, physical discomfort. The goal is not anxiety but the stripping away of fear. Once the worst outcome has been accepted, everything else becomes a bonus. This aligns with the core mission of this newsletter — actively facing what one has been avoiding.

## III. 💰 Investing Mindset — Rethinking the Nature of Money

"FU Money" is not an amount — it is a state: **when you can say "no" to anything you do not want, you are free.** For most people, this number is not the result of a financial independence calculation but a simple formula:

```
FU Money = Annual Basic Expenses × Risk Tolerance Years
```

If basic expenses are $30,000 per year and tolerable uncertainty is 3 years, then $90,000 is the threshold for the power to say no. Not retirement — **optionality.**

Technical professionals have a natural advantage in building "say no" capability, but often overlook a critical question: can these skills be converted into passive income? Not through selling courses or running ads, but through deeper transformations —

- Can accumulated workflows and automations become standalone products?
- Can a knowledge system become an investable asset, not just consumable content?
- Can the AI systems being trained create income, not just complete tasks?

These three questions matter a hundred times more than "which stock to buy."

Naval Ravikant's definition of wealth is worth revisiting: **wealth is not money — it is what creates value while you sleep.** Code is wealth. Content is not — unless distributed by code. A counterintuitive insight: because of technical expertise, there is a natural inclination to invest in tech stocks, and this is precisely what makes it dangerous — the industry one "knows" is mistaken for an industry one can "predict." The real edge lies in investing in businesses whose models are comprehensible but whose execution lies beyond technical capability — consumer goods, REITs, infrastructure. Using a technologist's perspective to spot undervalued traditional sectors — that is a genuine advantage.

- 📖 Morgan Housel, *The Psychology of Money* (a masterpiece)
- 📖 JL Collins, *The Simple Path to Wealth*
- 🎧 Podcast: *Invest Like the Best*
- 💡 A 5-minute action worth taking: calculate the FU Money number to know exactly how far away "the power to say no" really is.

## IV. 🧬 Unfamiliar Territory — Chronobiology

Inside every brain sits a "master clock" — the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) — which synchronizes the 24-hour rhythm through light exposure. But a key insight from the 2017 Nobel Prize research is this: **every organ in the body has its own independent clock.** The liver has a clock. Muscles have clocks. Even skin cells run on independent circadian rhythms. The master clock coordinates them, but each peripheral clock can be independently regulated by food intake, exercise, and light exposure. This is why "consistent sleep schedule" alone is insufficient — the right things need to happen at the right times.

A frequently overlooked fact: caffeine has a half-life of 5 to 6 hours. That coffee at 3 p.m. means half the caffeine is still blocking adenosine receptors at 9 p.m., reducing deep sleep quality by 20–30%. One slept, but did not truly recover.

Meal timing matters more than meal composition — the same calories consumed at 8 a.m. versus 10 p.m. have dramatically different effects on the body. The reason lies in the independent response mechanisms of peripheral clocks, not the simplistic notion that "eating late makes you gain weight."

Why does any of this matter? Because **the body is the only hardware that cannot be swapped out.** AI models, tools, strategies — all can be replaced at any time. But the body runs for a lifetime. Without understanding its operating rules, all productivity optimizations are built on sand.

- 📖 Satchin Panda, *The Circadian Code*
- 📄 2017 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine (Hall, Rosbash, Young)
- 🎯 An experiment worth trying: for three consecutive days, record when genuine creativity strikes — not the moments of "being busy," but the moments of "producing something good" — and see if a pattern emerges.

## V. 🌍 A Fresh Lens — AI Opportunities in the Silver Economy

China's population aged 60+ has surpassed 300 million, exceeding 21% of the total. By 2035, this number will surpass 400 million. **This is a market larger than the entire United States, yet almost no AI practitioners are taking it seriously.** Why? Because people building AI are overwhelmingly young and struggle to intuitively grasp the real needs of the elderly.

But stepping outside a purely technical perspective reveals opportunities everywhere:

- An AI companion that genuinely understands loneliness — not a Siri-style responder, but a conversational system that sustains emotional connection over time
- A chronic disease medication manager — timely reminders, symptom logging, automatic sync to distant family members
- An anti-fraud protection system for the elderly — call monitoring, scam pattern recognition, real-time alerts
- A digital legacy tool that organizes a lifetime of memories — photos, stories, audio — into a transmissible family archive

Japan's CareTech market is already taking off: SoftBank's Pepper robot leads exercise sessions in nursing homes, Panasonic is developing smart care beds, and Magic Leap founder Rony Abovitz's new venture Sun and Thunder is building AI companions specifically for elderly individuals living alone. In China, the reality is even more pressing: a caregiver shortage exceeding 10 million, extremely high rates of children living in different cities from their parents, and the "4-2-1 family structure" concentrating eldercare pressure on a tiny number of people. This is not a future opportunity — it is a current necessity.
