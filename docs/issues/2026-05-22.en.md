In January 2024, French archaeologist Stéphen Rostain published a paper in *Science* with an innocuous-sounding title. But what LiDAR and satellite imagery revealed made the archaeology world collectively catch its breath: beneath Ecuador's Upano Valley lay an entire networked urban civilization. Over six thousand raised platforms. An intricate road system. Grid-planned farmland. This wasn't a cluster of hunter-gatherer villages — this was a metropolitan civilization that emerged around 500 BC and thrived for two millennia, comparable in scale to Rome during the same period.

The catch? This civilization left behind no writing. Zero.

And that quietly dismantles our definition of "civilization." Textbooks say: civilization = writing + cities + metallurgy + complex social organization. If these Amazonians built cities of tens of thousands, managed complex agricultural systems, and sustained social order for two thousand years without a single written character — then what exactly gives us the right to say they weren't "civilized"? Your keyboard and your screen — maybe they're not as essential as you thought.

Rewind to 2009. A slight, elderly American woman stands at the Nobel Prize podium, breaking two records simultaneously: first woman to win the Economics prize, and the first person to reach the summit by studying *failure*. Elinor Ostrom spent her entire career trying to prove Garrett Hardin wrong.

In 1968, Hardin published his famous "Tragedy of the Commons" — arguing that shared resources are inevitably overexploited because every rational actor maximizes their own gain. The only solutions: privatization or government regulation. The argument was so seductive nobody wanted to challenge it. It fit the textbooks, fit our intuitions, and conveniently justified both big government and free markets. Very useful.

But Ostrom went to Törbel, a Swiss alpine village, and found that its herders had been collectively managing high-mountain pastures for over eight hundred years — no privatization, no government oversight, just a set of village rules. She went to Japanese forests. She went to the Water Tribunal of Valencia, Spain, which has been adjudicating irrigation disputes continuously for over a thousand years. She went to the Philippines, Nepal, Kenya. Her conclusion was simple: Hardin wasn't describing the "tragedy of the commons." He was describing the tragedy of *unmanaged open access*. These are not the same thing.

The eight design principles she distilled read today like a description of a healthy open-source community: clearly defined boundaries, rules that match local conditions, collective decision-making, effective monitoring, graduated sanctions, conflict resolution mechanisms, external recognition, and nested governance. Run those eight principles against the healthiest open-source projects on GitHub and the overlap is uncanny. Institutions aren't abstractions — they're just a group's consensus about "how we do things." And that consensus can emerge spontaneously. It doesn't need to be designed from the top down.

Speaking of unexpected connections: at the 1801 Paris Industrial Exhibition, a weaver named Joseph Marie Jacquard unveiled a machine. It could automatically weave astonishingly complex patterns — flowers, birds, landscapes — and the pattern controller was a chain of punched cards. Where there was a hole, the hook passed through. Where there wasn't, it was blocked. This was, in every meaningful sense, a binary control system.

Eighty years later, a young man named Herman Hollerith was helping the US Census Bureau process the 1890 census. He remembered Jacquard's punched cards, adapted them — not to control threads, but to record data. His tabulating machine would eventually become a company called IBM. And Jacquard-style punch cards remained in active use in computing well into the 1980s, until tape and disk finally replaced them.

Charles Babbage, conceiving his Analytical Engine in the 1830s, drew direct inspiration from Jacquard's cards. He even hung a Jacquard-woven portrait of Jacquard himself on his wall. Ada Lovelace — history's first programmer — wrote the first algorithm for that never-built engine, and she was fundamentally thinking about the same problem: can we control the progression of numbers the way a loom controls the progression of thread?

So every time you write code that controls a flow, you're doing something that weavers figured out in 1804. The logic of programming — input, condition, output — is older than computers. It grew out of the bones of a textile machine.

Now pull the lens all the way back. In the summer of 1950, physicist Enrico Fermi was at the Los Alamos lab lunch table when he suddenly asked: "Where is everybody?" The table went quiet, because everyone knew what he meant: if the galaxy has so many planets suitable for life, and if civilizations can achieve interstellar travel, why do we see nothing?

That silence is the Fermi Paradox. For decades, answers have accumulated: civilizations self-destruct (nuclear war, climate collapse, AI gone wrong); interstellar travel is simply too hard; or — the darkest interpretation — there's a "Great Filter" standing across every civilization's evolutionary path, and we haven't encountered it yet. Economist Robin Hanson formalized the Great Filter concept in a 1998 paper, and his conclusion was deeply unsettling: if we ever discover even the simplest microbial fossils on Mars or Europa, that's *bad news*. It would mean the origin of life wasn't the filter — so the filter lies ahead of us.

JWST data in 2024 has reignited this conversation. Exoplanet atmospheric spectroscopy grows ever more refined, but there's no sign of technosignatures anywhere. The universe is too quiet, and that quiet is starting to feel suspicious.

One last topic. This one's in your mouth.

Over 90% of the world's Camembert cheese is made with a single fungal strain. *Penicillium camemberti*, the white mold that gives soft-ripened cheese its rind, was once a grey-green wild mold that humans spent centuries selecting and domesticating into the cheese-making workhorse it is today. The problem: it has lost the ability to reproduce sexually. It is, in effect, a billion identical clones.

Researchers at France's National Center for Scientific Research raised the alarm in late 2023: if this strain suddenly collapses — a matching bacteriophage, an environmental shock — Camembert and Brie would vanish from the Earth. Not get more expensive. Not become rare. Vanish. There's no backup. No wild relative to "roll back" to.

This isn't hypothetical. The Gros Michel banana — the only banana the world ate before the 1950s — was wiped out by a fungal disease called Panama disease. The Cavendish banana, its replacement, now faces a variant strain of the same pathogen. Cheese. Bananas. Coffee. Chocolate. The more we pursue standardization and efficiency, the more we cover the global supply chain with monocultures — the closer we skate to "suddenly, nothing."

You might think this is an agriculture problem. But think about your work: if your entire tech stack depends on a single open-source library, and that library's maintainer burns out — you know this feeling.
