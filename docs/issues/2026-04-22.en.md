## I. 🧠 Memory Is Migrating from Internal to External

In 2011, Columbia University's Betsy Sparrow ran a famous experiment. Participants read trivia, then half were told it was saved to a computer. Result: those told "it's saved" remembered the content worse — but remembered *exactly which folder it was stored in*.

This is the Google Effect: when the brain knows information is retrievable externally, it offloads memory and keeps only the retrieval path. Years later, this effect has expanded from search to entire digital life — notes, AI knowledge bases, agent memory.

## II. 📉 Three Hidden Costs

**Cost 1: Creativity depends on recombination.** True creativity is the cross-domain recombination of memory fragments. "Connecting the dots" — requires dots in your head. External storage has no "shower moment."

**Cost 2: Judgment depends on memory precision.** An AI-summarized document — you remember the summary, not the details the AI deemed "unimportant." Those details may be exactly what matters.

**Cost 3: Identity erosion.** Who you are depends on what you remember. When everything is in external systems, "I have notes" replaces "I know."

## III. 🔄 Two Principles

**Principle 1: Reconstruct in your own words before storing.** Not highlight, not copy-paste, not AI summary. This is the core of the Zettelkasten method — value is in connection, not storage.

**Principle 2: Monthly memory blackout.** Turn off all retrieval tools. Try to recall the month's key information and decisions. Calibrate your sense of what you actually know.

- 📄 Sparrow, Liu & Wegner (2011). Google Effects on Memory. *Science*
