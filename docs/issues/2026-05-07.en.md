## I. 🧵 The Truth About Prompt Engineering

Prompt engineering is widely discussed as "the skill of talking to AI." But its real essence: every token you input adjusts a trillion-parameter probability distribution. You're doing probability navigation through language — not "commanding," not "programming."

This reveals a fundamental limit: the AI can only understand what you can describe in language. And language has its own natural boundary.

This boundary was marked by Wittgenstein in 1921: **"The limits of my language mean the limits of my world."** A century later, this has become a technical constraint for AI engineering — no matter how large your model or how long your context, what it can understand will never exceed what you can describe in language.

## II. 🚧 The Three Shackles of Language

**First: The unsayable.** The smell of a flower, the bodily response to a favorite song, the feel of the first sip of hot coffee — these experiences cannot be fully described in language. Not for lack of vocabulary, but because experience itself exists outside the linguistic system. You can't make an AI understand "what the first sip of coffee feels like" — you can only describe, and description is never experience.

**Second: Contextual load.** When you tell a colleague "let's go with last time's approach," they understand. Give that to an AI and it doesn't know what "last time" is or which "approach." About 70% of information in human communication is in context, not in words. Prompts must make all hidden context explicit — and what you can make explicit is always just the tip of the iceberg.

**Third: Self-reference paradox.** The deepest cognition often points to itself. When you try to describe "how I think" in language, the description itself changes the thinking. The same with prompts: every prompt optimization redefines what you want. Between what you write in the prompt — "I need factual, data-driven answers" — and what you actually expect, lies a gap language cannot bridge.

## III. 🔮 From Better Prompts to Better Calibration

The endgame of prompt engineering isn't better templates — it's **releasing the over-trust in language.** Instead of spending two hours polishing a prompt:

1. Multi-turn iteration over single-shot perfection. Let the AI output first, then refine based on results
2. Examples over descriptions. Three perfect outputs define the probability distribution more effectively than any description
3. Accept the unsayable. AI is not your doppelgänger — it's a mirror built from language and probability. What it reflects is what you could say. What you couldn't — keep for yourself.

- 📖 Ludwig Wittgenstein, *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus*
