## I. 🎢 A Nobel Prize Discovery

In 1996, Daniel Kahneman asked two groups to hold their hands in ice water. Group A: 14°C for 60 seconds. Group B: same 14°C for 60 seconds, then temperature silently rose to 15°C for 30 more seconds.

Objectively, Group B suffered more (30 extra seconds). Yet when asked which they'd rather repeat, most chose Group B. Why?

Because memory of experience is not the average of all moments — it's the average of **the peak (most intense moment) and the end.** Group B's ending was slightly milder, and that was enough to make the entire experience "feel better" in memory. Kahneman won the Nobel Prize for this.

## II. 🔍 How It Shapes Every Day

The Peak-End Rule means your judgment of life quality isn't based on "how was it, on average" — but on "what was the peak, and how did it end."

- A trip's memory depends on the best day and the last day, not the average
- A relationship's evaluation depends on the sweetest moment and the breakup
- A product's reputation depends on the first wow and the final feel

## III. ⚡ Using It in Content Creation

This isn't manipulation — it's understanding how memory actually works, then using it honestly:

**To raise the peak:** use concrete numbers, share personal vulnerable stories, create cognitive conflict.
**To raise the ending:** give an instantly actionable instruction, or leave a line worth rereading.

- 📖 Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Chapter 25)
- 💡 Experiment: review your last three pieces of content. Mark the "peak paragraph" and "last sentence." Ask: is the peak high enough? Does the ending land?
