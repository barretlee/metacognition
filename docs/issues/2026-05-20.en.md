## I. 🧬 Fear Can Be Inherited — And You Might Be Paying Your Grandfather's Debt

In January 2026, *Mammalian Genome* published a major review with a title that reads like a manifesto: *"Genetic Heritage of War: Exploring Transgenerational Inheritance of PTSD and Its Molecular Background."* Authors Džaferović and Ašić synthesized decades of research to reach a startling conclusion: **stress can genuinely be written into DNA and passed to the next generation.**

This isn't a simple revival of Lamarckism — the discredited idea that giraffes stretching their necks produces longer-necked offspring. The mechanism is far more precise. When a pregnant woman experiences extreme stress — war, famine, violence — her body releases cortisol. These hormones don't just affect her own cells. They cross the placenta and alter the fetus's **DNA methylation patterns** — adding or removing methyl tags on key genes, like sticky notes on a DNA sequence, deciding which genes get read and which get silenced.

The most commonly tagged genes include FKBP5 (regulates stress hormone response), NR3C1 (encodes glucocorticoid receptors), and BDNF (affects neuroplasticity). These tags persist after birth and may carry forward when those children become parents. A separate 2026 article in *Frontiers in Psychiatry* has already begun exploring **therapeutic reversibility** — if trauma can be epigenetically acquired, perhaps healing can be too.

The most famous case is the Dutch Hunger Winter. During the Nazi blockade of 1944-45, western Netherlands experienced severe famine. Scientists at Columbia University tracked children born to mothers pregnant during the famine and found that as adults, they had significantly higher rates of obesity, cardiovascular disease, and mental illness. More striking: **their children** — the grandchildren of famine survivors — were also born with lower birth weights. One environmental shock, three generations of bodies remembering.

What does this mean for a tech worker staring at screens all day? The chronic anxiety you feel isn't necessarily from too many Slack notifications or KPI pressure. Part of it may come from a famine eighty years ago, a war, something a great-grandparent was forced to endure — and the **chemical imprint** of that experience is still circulating in your cells.

- 📖 *Frontiers in Psychiatry* (2026): "Epigenetic Changes Associated with Multi-Generational Trauma"
- 📖 Nessa Carey, *The Epigenetics Revolution* (2011) — the best popular introduction to epigenetics
- 🎧 Radiolab podcast: *Inheritance* (three-part series on the biological evidence for transgenerational trauma)
- 🎯 **Thought experiment**: If you start meditating or exercising today, you're not just changing your own cortisol levels — you might be altering the baseline stress response of children who haven't been born yet. Sit with that for a moment.

## II. 🏗️ The Giant Dam on the Nile — When International Law Goes Silent Before $4 Billion

On the Ethiopian highlands sits a dam 1,780 meters long, costing $4 billion. The **Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD)**, officially inaugurated in September 2025, is Africa's largest hydroelectric project. For Ethiopia — a nation of 120 million — it's a symbol of national renewal, promising stable electricity and the ability to export power to neighbors.

But for downstream Egypt, this dam is seen as an **existential threat.** Egypt gets 97% of its freshwater from the Nile, and the Nile gets 86% of its water from the Ethiopian highlands. If Ethiopia hoards too much water behind its dam, Egypt — a desert nation of 110 million — faces catastrophic water shortages.

In January 2026, U.S. President Trump offered to mediate. But the problem goes deeper than diplomacy. In April 2026, *JURIST* legal magazine published a commentary titled *"Ethiopia, Egypt, and the Nile: Why International Water Law Is Failing the GERD Dispute,"* identifying the core dilemma: **the legal framework itself is fragmented.** The 1997 UN Watercourses Convention establishes principles of "equitable and reasonable utilization" and "obligation not to cause significant harm" — noble ideas, but with virtually no enforcement mechanism. Ethiopia never signed the convention. Egypt's claim to "historic water rights" rests on colonial-era treaties from 1929 and 1959 — treaties Ethiopia never recognized.

This is international law at its most awkward: **when both sides have sufficient national will, law degrades into rhetorical weaponry at the negotiating table.** Principles without enforcement are just polite suggestions.

GERD is merely one node in a global pattern. The Indus (India-Pakistan), the Mekong (China-Southeast Asia), the Tigris-Euphrates (Turkey-Iraq-Syria) — every transboundary river is becoming a potential flashpoint under the twin pressures of climate change and population growth. Scientists in 2026 warn: dam failure or mismanagement could cause catastrophic flooding in Sudan and Egypt. This isn't a drill.

- 📖 *JURIST* (April 22, 2026): Mostafa Ahmed Fouad Makled, "Ethiopia, Egypt, and the Nile"
- 📖 *Mongabay* (Feb 6, 2026): "Ethiopia's Renaissance Mega-Dam Fuels Energy Hopes and Regional Anxiety"
- 🎧 *BBC The Inquiry* — "Is the World Running Out of Water?" — global water stress beyond the Nile
- 🎯 **Connect to your domain**: The GERD dispute has structural similarities to open-source license governance — how much enforcement power do non-binding license terms (GPL, MIT) actually have against sufficiently powerful commercial interests? The answer may be the same in both cases: they work when power is balanced, and become decorative when it isn't.

## III. 💰 Donor-Advised Funds (DAFs) — America's Latest "Give-and-Dodge" Machine

If you're a reasonably well-compensated tech worker or founder, you'll eventually face a question: **you've made money — now what about the taxes?**

In the United States — a country with over a century of fine-grained tax optimization — something interesting happened in 2026. Congress passed a bill called the OBBB (Opportunity for a Better Budget Bill), now in effect. It did something unprecedented: **for the first time, all taxpayers — including the ~90% who take the standard deduction — can deduct up to $1,000 ($2,000 for married couples) in charitable donations.**

The policy is framed as encouraging philanthropy, but the people who will really exploit it aren't ordinary taxpayers. They're the high-net-worth individuals who already push tax avoidance to its limits — and their tool of choice is the **Donor-Advised Fund (DAF).**

Here's how a DAF works, and it's elegant enough to feel like a bug. You contribute assets — cash, stock, even cryptocurrency — to a DAF account and **immediately** receive the full charitable tax deduction. But the money doesn't have to go to actual operating charities right away. It can sit in the DAF, continue to grow through investment, and you can designate specific charitable recipients at any future time — next year, a decade from now, or even after your death. Technically you've "donated." The tax deduction is booked. But actual control remains in your hands.

The problem: this creates a **philanthropic capital black hole.** An estimated $230 billion sits stranded in American DAFs — having completed the tax-avoidance workflow but never flowing to the charities that actually need it. In late 2025, the U.S. Treasury released proposed regulations targeting DAFs, trying to close loopholes — particularly around DAF-to-DAF transfers.

For tech workers outside the U.S., a DAF isn't a directly applicable tool (different tax codes). But the underlying logic is worth absorbing: **philanthropy is a specialized financial product.** Its core value isn't just "doing good" — it's "doing good while legally reducing tax liability." This way of thinking — financializing moral behavior — is precisely the cognitive blind spot most prevalent in the Chinese tech community. When you're thinking about "how to handle stock option taxes" or "how to structure overseas income," what you might be missing isn't a better accountant — it's a mental framework that integrates philanthropy into your overall financial strategy.

- 📖 GiveChariot (2026): "How the New Tax Bill Will Reshape Philanthropy and DAFs"
- 📖 Choate Law — Treasury DAF proposed regulations analysis (2025)
- 📖 Ray D. Madoff, *Immortality and the Law: The Rising Power of the American Dead* — sharp critique of DAFs and philanthropic capital stagnation
- 🎯 **This issue's action**: Spend 15 minutes researching the charitable deduction policy in your jurisdiction. Even if you use the standard deduction, there may be pathways. Helping others while legally reducing your tax burden isn't "gaming the system" — it's understanding the rules and operating within them.

## IV. 📚 Borges's Infinite Library — And Why 2026 Is the Perfect Year to Read Him

June 14, 2026, marks the **40th anniversary of the death of Jorge Luis Borges.**

If you've never read Borges: he was one of the most imaginative writers of the 20th century — Argentine, blind for the latter half of his life, a librarian by profession. His work isn't traditional fiction — it's closer to **thought experiments wrapped in prose.** He wrote about a book with infinite pages (*The Book of Sand*), about a library containing every possible book (*The Library of Babel*), about an empire that drew a map exactly the size of the empire itself (*On Exactitude in Science*).

In February 2026, *El País* reported that lectures Borges gave in 1966 at the Catholic University of Argentina — on English and American literature — have been collected and published as a new book: *Curso de literatura inglesa y norteamericana.* The content came from oral recordings, never before in book form. The article put it beautifully: "Borges's works seem to mimic *The Book of Sand* — decades after his death, new pages keep being added."

Geneva — where Borges lived his final years and died — is hosting **"Borges 2026: Geneva's Tribute to Literary Genius."** Meanwhile, Benengeli 2026, the international Spanish-language literary festival, is running events across five continents with Borges as a central figure of homage.

Why should a technologist care about a blind writer who died 40 years ago? Two reasons.

First, **Borges's writing is fundamentally about information, infinity, and representation — precisely the core problems of the AI era.** His *Library of Babel* describes a universe consisting of all possible books — all permutations of 25 characters. This library contains every version of Shakespeare, every possible translation error, and one book that proves "this book does not exist." This is, logically, what a language model does when sampling from the distribution of all possible token sequences. Borges was thinking about what transformers do today — in 1941.

Second, **his blindness is a cognitive metaphor.** Borges went blind in 1955, and every subsequent work was dictated, recorded by others. A man who couldn't see the world created the 20th century's richest imaginative universe — which is itself a refutation of "sensory input = creativity ceiling." In an era where text perception increasingly depends on the visual (charts, video, UI), Borges reminds us: **the best imagination happens when senses are constrained, not when they're saturated.**

- 📖 Jorge Luis Borges, *The Garden of Forking Paths* (1941) — start here
- 📖 Borges, *The Book of Sand* (1975) — the infinite book
- 📄 *El País* (Feb 2, 2026): "The Lost Lessons of Jorge Luis Borges" — on the 2026 discovery
- 🎯 **This issue's action**: Read one Borges short story — 15 minutes. Recommended: *Funes the Memorious* (about a man who can't forget anything) or *The Library of Babel*. After reading, ask yourself: is what Borges described in the 1940s the same philosophical problem that LLMs pose today?

## V. 🔬 Teaching Fungi to Speak Wittgenstein — The Newest Life of Language Game Theory

On May 5, 2026, an unusual paper appeared on arXiv. First author Yanbo Zhang, collaborating with **Michael Levin** — the Tufts University bioelectricity researcher who made headlines by showing that flatworm memories can be transplanted via electrical patterns, and by making frogs grow extra eyes.

The paper's title: *"Language Game: Talking to Non-Human Systems,"* directly invoking Wittgenstein's core concept. The central thesis is bold enough to sound absurd and yet serious enough to be on arXiv: **perhaps we don't need LLMs to "translate" non-human systems (gene regulatory networks, microbial consortia, fungal mycelial networks) — perhaps we can directly "play language games" with them.**

Wittgenstein's concept of "language games" comes from his late masterpiece *Philosophical Investigations* (1953). The gist: **meaning doesn't reside in reference (the word "apple" points to an apple) — it resides in use (the word "apple" means entirely different things when ordering food, describing a color, or naming a company).** You don't fail to understand a word because you haven't seen its definition — you fail because you haven't participated in the "game" where that word is used.

Zhang and Levin push this to the limit. Their framework: take a non-human system (say, a gene regulatory network or a slime mold) and use its internal dynamics as the policy core for reinforcement learning, then through "play" — rewards, feedback, interaction — let it learn to communicate with humans. Not "translating" its language but **co-creating a new language with it.**

This sounds like science fiction, but Levin's prior experiments have already shown: cells' bioelectric patterns encode morphological information ("how many eyes to grow," "which direction the body should face"), and these patterns are readable and writable. If cells already have an "information layer," they could potentially participate in "language games" — if you find the right mode of interaction.

For technologists, the real impact of this paper isn't "can we chat with bacteria." It's that it fundamentally questions a hidden assumption of the LLM paradigm: **we assume "meaning" must be mediated through human language.** If Wittgenstein was right — meaning only exists in use, not in symbols themselves — then a GPT model outputting "I love you" and a fungal mycelial network extending in a particular direction are, from a semantic standpoint, potentially **meaning-acts at the same level.** They're each participating in their respective language games, and we've simply gotten used to one of them.

- 📄 arXiv:2605.16321 (May 2026): Zhang & Levin, "Language Game: Talking to Non-Human Systems"
- 📖 *Journal of NeuroPhilosophy*: "From Wittgenstein's Language Games to LLMs: Representation, Meaning..."
- 📖 Ludwig Wittgenstein, *Philosophical Investigations* (1953) — you don't need to finish it; Part I alone is explosive
- 🎧 Podcast: Michael Levin on *Lex Fridman Podcast* (#325) — on bioelectricity, morphogenesis, and where "meaning" lives in biology
- 🎯 **This issue's action**: Next time you write a prompt, try a mental inversion — not "how do I get the AI to understand my intent more accurately," but "what language game are the AI and I playing? What are the rules? Who made them?" When you start treating prompt design as "game rule design" rather than "translation task," your entire approach will shift.
